# Labour Market Tightness Research
## Are the V/U Band Labels Defensible? A Cross-Validated Assessment

**Date:** 2026-05-09
**Tier:** Tier 2 (autonomous research; not user-reviewed)
**Scope:** Research into whether the framework's V/U band labels — anchored to employer-side narrative signals — are defensible against supply-side cross-validation, international comparisons, and critical academic literature

---

## Executive Summary

The framework's V/U band labels ("approaching balance / starting to be tight" at 0.45–0.60, "exceptionally tight" at >0.80) derive exclusively from employer-side anchors: BoC BOS labour shortage percentages, and BoC Governor/Deputy Governor speeches citing business complaints about unfilled positions. This research examined whether four cross-validation routes — supply-side worker signals, international comparisons, critical Canadian academic literature, and the immigration-policy feedback loop — corroborate those anchors.

**Verdict: Option A+B is most defensible.** The 0.45–0.60 band label ("starting to be tight") deserves relabelling to something more epistemically honest (e.g., "elevated demand pressure / employers reporting shortages"), and the prose for all bands should cross-check whether wage growth confirms or qualifies the tightness signal. The labels are not fabricated — they reflect real institutional signals — but those signals have now been documented to be systematically employer-biased, subject to status-quo effects, and not validated by worker-side outcomes at the time they were cited. The BoC's own Deputy Governor acknowledged in the same Jan 2019 speech that wage growth was "shy of what one would expect in a tight labour market."

## 1. Canadian Academic and Grey Literature

### 1.1 Bank of Canada's Own Research

**SAN 2022-18 — Lam (November 2022): "Canada's Beveridge curve and the outlook for the labour market"**
[bankofcanada.ca/2022/11/staff-analytical-note-2022-18/](https://www.bankofcanada.ca/2022/11/staff-analytical-note-2022-18/)

The BoC's own analysis at the peak of the 2022 episode projected that if job vacancy rates returned to pre-pandemic levels, unemployment would rise to about 6.7% — "far below what has historically been observed during recessions." Importantly, the analysis noted the Beveridge curve had "largely reversed" the outward shift observed at pandemic onset. The abstract: "Canada's labour market is tight but beginning to ease. Unemployment will likely rise in turn, but the economy can avoid a recessionary surge given current conditions." This is not a strong endorsement of the "exceptionally tight" framing; it is a forward-looking soft-landing baseline.

**SAN 2024-8 — Benchmarks for assessing labour market health: 2024 update**
[bankofcanada.ca/2024/04/staff-analytical-note-2024-8/](https://www.bankofcanada.ca/2024/04/staff-analytical-note-2024-8/)

By 2024, the BoC's own benchmarks showed the labour market had "likely entered into balance from overheated levels," with the key evidence being "large reduction in job openings and other demand indicators but no significant rise in layoffs." This framing — normalisation via vacancy decline rather than layoffs — is relevant to the immigration story below: the excess was absorbed by slowing hiring, not firing. The note also showed wage growth had moderated to "below 4%," consistent with reduced labour pressure.

**SAN 2025-17 — Benchmarks for assessing labour market health: 2025 update**
[bankofcanada.ca/2025/06/staff-analytical-note-2025-17/](https://www.bankofcanada.ca/2025/06/staff-analytical-note-2025-17/)

The 2025 update explicitly identifies immigration supply expansion as a diagnostic factor: "strong population growth during 2023 and most of 2024 increased the size of the labour force." The note documents modest excess supply as of 2025, with vacancy rates having "softened considerably" while wage growth "remain[s] elevated," a divergence the note explicitly notes rather than collapsing into a single tightness reading. The multi-indicator framework treats BOS labour shortage signals, vacancy rates, and wage measures as separate channels — it does not treat BOS as the tightness headline.

**Implication for framework:** The BoC does not itself privilege the BOS labour shortage signal as the headline tightness measure. It uses it as one of several inputs. Our framework's use of Wilkins Jan 2019 (BOS-based) and Macklem Nov 2022 (BOS + vacancies combined) as band anchors is internally consistent with BoC communications — but those speeches do not constitute the BoC endorsing specific V/U threshold numbers as canonical.

**BoC SAN 2022-10 — "Has wage setting changed in Canada? Evidence from the pre-pandemic 2020 Wage-Setting Survey"**
[bankofcanada.ca/2022/08/staff-analytical-note-2022-10/](https://www.bankofcanada.ca/2022/08/staff-analytical-note-2022-10/)

Examines whether wage-setting behaviour shifted post-pandemic. Relevant context: the survey captured firms' own assessments of labour conditions alongside wage outcomes — a potential source for triangulating employer perception vs wage-setting action.

### 1.2 The Wilkins Jan 2019 Anchor — What She Actually Said

**BoC speech: "A Look Under the Hood of Canada's Job Market" (Senior Deputy Governor Wilkins, January 31, 2019)**
[bankofcanada.ca/2019/01/look-under-hood-canadas-job-market/](https://www.bankofcanada.ca/2019/01/look-under-hood-canadas-job-market/)

This is the speech anchoring the 0.45–0.60 "starting to be tight" band. The full context materially changes the interpretive weight:

- Exact BOS quote cited: "Firms in our latest Business Outlook Survey reported one of the highest levels of labour shortages since the Great Recession." (**Verified.**)
- Wilkins' own characterisation of wage growth at the same moment: wages had "picked up considerably to average about 2½ per cent in 2018" but remained **"shy of what one would expect in a tight labour market."**
- She then asked explicitly: **"If the right workers are so hard to attract, why not simply offer more money?"** — and answered that "other factors must be holding back wages."
- She cited skills mismatches, low job churn/turnover, geographic barriers, and **structural factors reducing workers' bargaining power** (concentration of firm market power, gig economy) as explanations for the wage-growth gap.
- She described the neutral unemployment rate estimate as "like dark matter in physics" — "subject to great uncertainty."

**This is the most critical finding of this research.** The very speech that anchors the "starting to be tight" band contains an explicit acknowledgement that employer-reported shortages did not translate to the wage pressure the framework uses as its implied validation. Wilkins was simultaneously citing employer complaints at record levels AND noting that wages were not responding as tightness theory would predict. The user's status-quo-bias hypothesis — that employers accustomed to a large unemployed pool read any competition for workers as "shortage" — is directly corroborated by the BoC's own diagnostic: something was "holding back wages" other than labour abundance.

### 1.3 Statistics Canada Research

**StatsCan Economic Insights: "Real wages and productivity during the COVID-19 pandemic" (2022)**
[statcan.gc.ca/n1/pub/36-28-0001/2022010/article/00002-eng.htm](https://www150.statcan.gc.ca/n1/pub/36-28-0001/2022010/article/00002-eng.htm)

During the peak employer-reported shortage period (2021-Q2 2022), workers' real wages (CPI-deflated) rose only 3.6% — limited gains driven by external factors (rising export prices, housing costs) rather than productivity-linked compensation growth. Using the GDP deflator (the firm's perspective), real wages were actually **7.1% lower** in Q2 2022 vs Q1 2019. The tight labour market did not translate to proportional real wage benefits for workers.

**StatsCan Research to Insights: "Wages in Canada, 1981 to 2024" (2025)**
[statcan.gc.ca/n1/pub/11-631-x/11-631-x2025003-eng.htm](https://www150.statcan.gc.ca/n1/pub/11-631-x/11-631-x2025003-eng.htm)

The wage growth posted between 2019 and 2024 was "almost exclusively driven by gains among a specific group of workers in management, natural and applied sciences and business and finance." Low-wage workers showed limited evidence of robust bargaining power. Young full-time workers aged 17-24 showed an increase in low-paid job share from 2019 to 2023, directly contradicting the worker-side tightness hypothesis (that genuine tightness should narrow the low-pay proportion).

### 1.4 C.D. Howe Institute

**C.D. Howe Commentary 648: "Empty Seats: Why Labour Shortages Plague Small and Medium-Sized Businesses"**
[cdhowe.org/publication/empty-seats-why-labour-shortages-plague-small-and-medium-sized-businesses/](https://cdhowe.org/publication/empty-seats-why-labour-shortages-plague-small-and-medium-sized-businesses/)

Identifies structural causes of vacancy persistence in SMEs that are unrelated to economy-wide tightness: inability to offer benefits competitive with large firms, geographic labour market fragmentation, and credential mismatches. These sectoral/structural explanations for elevated BOS shortage readings are not captured in aggregate V/U.

**C.D. Howe: "Why More Immigration Didn't Solve Canada's Labour Shortages"**
[cdhowe.org/publication/why-more-immigration-didnt-solve-canadas-labour-shortages/](https://cdhowe.org/publication/why-more-immigration-didnt-solve-canadas-labour-shortages/)

The immigration supply increase failed to bring vacancy rates down — partly because immigrants simultaneously expanded consumption and labour demand (Fortin's fallacy-of-composition finding, below). More than half a million workers were underemployed in 2024 despite "high full-time vacancies," indicating mismatches rather than genuine scarcity.

**C.D. Howe: "2024 Labour Market Review" (Mahboubi and Zhang)**
[cdhowe.org/publication/2024-labour-market-review-challenges-trends-and-policy-solutions-for-canada/](https://cdhowe.org/publication/2024-labour-market-review-challenges-trends-and-policy-solutions-for-canada/)

The rising unemployment rate in 2024 was "primarily driven by a mismatch between labour force expansion and job creation rather than a decline in labour demand." Calls for raising the retirement age, improving training programs, and easing credential recognition — structural supply-mismatch fixes, not demand-side tightness responses.

### 1.5 Pierre Fortin (UQAM) — Regional Beveridge Curves

**CLEF Working Paper 070-2024: "Does Immigration Help Alleviate Economy-Wide Labour Shortages?" (Fortin, May 2024)**
[clef.uwaterloo.ca/wp-content/uploads/2024/05/CLEF-070-2024-EN.pdf](https://clef.uwaterloo.ca/wp-content/uploads/2024/05/CLEF-070-2024-EN.pdf)

**Also published as C.D. Howe commentary: "The Immigration Paradox: How an Influx of Newcomers Has Led to Labour Shortages"**
[cdhowe.org/publication/the-immigration-paradox-how-an-influx-of-newcomers-has-led-to-labour-shortages/](https://cdhowe.org/publication/the-immigration-paradox-how-an-influx-of-newcomers-has-led-to-labour-shortages/)

Fortin's regional Beveridge curve analysis (six Canadian regions, 2015–2024) is the most directly relevant Canadian academic work to this research question. Key findings:

1. **Matching efficiency, not demand**: From 2019 to 2024, matching efficiency dropped ~20% below its pre-pandemic level and did not recover. Labour reallocation intensity also fell ~15% below 2019 levels. The net effect: the Beveridge curve sat at a **higher level in 2024 than in 2019** (job vacancy rate ~3.2% vs ~2.8% at the same unemployment rate), meaning a given V/U corresponds to *less* genuine scarcity than the same V/U pre-pandemic.

2. **The fallacy of composition**: "The common-sense belief that more immigration contributes to reducing economy-wide labour scarcity is wrong and constitutes a dangerous fallacy of composition." Individual employers benefited from immigrant workers, but aggregate vacancy rates worsened. Non-permanent residents increased from 1.4% of the labour force (2015) to 3.2% (2023); the job vacancy rate rose from ~2.8% to ~4.1% over the same period.

3. **Regional heterogeneity**: Western provinces (Ontario to BC) experienced larger immigration increases *and* larger vacancy rate increases than Eastern provinces — contradicting the employer-shortage-relief narrative at the regional level.

4. **Fortin explicitly flags** that the analysis treats vacancy rates as the primary indicator without examining whether "genuine" tightness manifested as wage growth. This is a limitation of his own work, and it applies to our framework's band labels equally.

**Implication**: Even in 2022, when V/U was at ~0.99 and employers were loudly reporting shortages, the Beveridge curve was shifted outward due to matching inefficiency — meaning the vacancy signal overstated true market tightness relative to what a pre-pandemic calibration would imply. The BoC's own SAN 2022-18 (Lam) noted the curve had "largely reversed" its initial outward shift, but post-pandemic matching efficiency degradation persisted into 2023-2024 per Fortin.

### 1.6 CCPA / David Macdonald and Jim Stanford

**CCPA: "Show Me the Money: It's Not a Worker Shortage, It's a Wage Shortage" (Macdonald, September 5, 2022)**
[policyalternatives.ca/news-research/show-me-the-money-its-not-a-worker-shortage-its-a-wage-shortage/](https://www.policyalternatives.ca/news-research/show-me-the-money-its-not-a-worker-shortage-its-a-wage-shortage/)

Written at the peak of the 2022 vacancy surge. Macdonald's data: in July 2022, there were 1.09 million unemployed Canadians and 1.04 million job vacancies — near V/U parity. He found that 63% of job postings were in industries where the wage offered was below the wage workers demanded to accept:
- Accommodation/food services: employers offered $15.85/hour; workers' minimum acceptable was $18.84/hour.
- Retail trade: posted $17.85/hour; workers required $23/hour minimum.

Macdonald's framing: "These industries aren't suffering from worker shortages, they are suffering from employers who are unwilling to increase wages enough to make it worth a worker's while." For most of the 2010s, the job-seeker-to-vacancy ratio was 6:1; by summer 2022 it had compressed to 1:1 — so employer complaints escalated even as the structural basis for "shortage" shifted dramatically from genuine scarcity to wage-gap unwillingness.

**HR Reporter / Jim Stanford (May 2023): "Is Canada's Labour Shortage a Myth?"**
[hrreporter.com/focus-areas/recruitment-and-staffing/is-canadas-labour-shortage-a-myth/376392](https://www.hrreporter.com/focus-areas/recruitment-and-staffing/is-canadas-labour-shortage-a-myth/376392)

Stanford (Centre for Future Work): "The fact that the so-called labour shortages are most severe in low-wage industries with relatively few formal requirements suggest it may be a problem with the jobs — not a shortage of people." Canada had ~1 million officially unemployed, ~1 million underemployed, and ~1 million who had stopped searching. Positions requiring lower qualifications had the *highest* vacancy rates — the opposite of a genuine skills-scarcity story.

**Analytical note on Macdonald and Stanford's framing**: Both are progressive-labour-side commentators and their wage-floor comparisons use individual willingness-to-accept measures that don't capture the full complexity of matching. Their core empirical point — that vacancies were concentrated in low-wage sectors that were not raising wages materially — is corroborated by StatsCan's own data on sector-specific vacancy rates and by Scotiabank and Indeed Hiring Lab wage data. The framing as "myth" is overstated; there was genuine post-COVID demand spike. But the component of employer-reported shortage that reflects wage-offer stubbornness rather than actual worker scarcity is real and well-documented.

### 1.7 Mikal Skuterud (Waterloo)

**The Hub interview: "Can Canada handle half a million new immigrants? Labour economist Mikal Skuterud on immigration's diminishing economic returns"**
[thehub.ca/podcast/audio/can-canada-handle-half-a-million-new-immigrants/](https://thehub.ca/podcast/audio/can-canada-handle-half-a-million-new-immigrants-labour-economist-mikal-skuterud-on-immigrations-diminishing-economic-returns/)

Skuterud's core argument: **"Labour demand is endogenous to labour supply."** Where particular worker types become plentiful, wages stay depressed; this fails to incentivize investment in training or automation. He explicitly rejects the premise that employer-reported labour shortages should drive immigration levels — the economy *adjusts* to available labour supply rather than having a fixed demand curve waiting to be filled. Illustrated with international comparisons: Florida has hand car washing (labour-abundant), Canada has automated car washes (labour-scarcer); Australia uses machines for grape harvests, California uses manual labour.

Skuterud does not provide a direct empirical assessment of the 2022 V/U ratio or whether it overstated tightness. His contribution is the structural argument: the theoretical premise underlying the framework's employer-shortage anchor is questionable. Employer labour demand is not a fixed quantity independent of wage rates and labour availability — it's endogenous. A "shortage" in this frame is partly an artefact of employers' expectations about the wage they should have to pay.

## 2. Supply-Side Worker-Side Signals

### 2.1 Real Wages — The Critical Test

If the labour market was genuinely tight (in the textbook sense of workers having bargaining power), real wages should have risen materially, especially in the sectors where employers were loudest about shortages.

**The data contradicts this for 2019 and complicates it for 2022:**

- **2018–2019 (the Wilkins "starting to be tight" anchor period):** Nominal wages grew ~2.5%. CPI was ~2%. Real wage growth was approximately flat. As Wilkins herself noted, this was "shy of what one would expect in a tight labour market."
- **2022 (the Macklem "exceptionally tight" anchor period):** Nominal wages grew ~4–5% (LFS) but CPI hit ~6.8%. Real wages fell ~1.4% in 2022. Workers were *losing* purchasing power at the same time employers were reporting record shortages. This is internally contradictory under the classical tightness definition.
- **Recovery:** Real wages finally turned positive in 2023 (+2%) and 2024 (+2.1%) as inflation fell, but by then the V/U ratio had already collapsed from 0.99 to approximately 0.3 by 2024–2025.

Source: Scotiabank Economics ([scotiabank.com/ca/en/about/economics/economics-publications](https://www.scotiabank.com/ca/en/about/economics/economics-publications/post.other-publications.insights-views.canadian-labour-market--august-21--2023-.html)); StatsCan real wage/productivity paper (op cit); RBC Economics.

**Key implication**: In the 2022 episode, the V/U ratio at ~0.99 (>0.80 "exceptionally tight") coincided with workers experiencing negative real wage growth. Under a genuine tightness framework, V/U near 1 should produce clear real wage gains. The fact that it did not suggests either (a) the Beveridge curve outward shift (matching inefficiency) meant less actual competition for workers than V/U implied, (b) institutional factors (weak unions, immigration expectations suppressing wage demands, cost-of-living political pressure) held nominal wages below what pure supply-demand would predict, or (c) a combination.

### 2.2 Wage Growth Distribution — Broad-Based or Narrow?

Indeed Hiring Lab Canada tracked posted wage growth, peaking at 5.3% Y/Y in mid-2022. By August 2023, posted wage growth had cooled to ~3.9%, with the moderation "more sharp in mid-paying occupations." Low-wage retail and customer service sectors held above 5% Y/Y growth through summer 2023 — consistent with employers in those sectors having to raise offered wages to attract workers in an era when inflation was also running hot.

StatsCan's "Wages in Canada 1981–2024" (op cit): the 2019-2024 gains were "almost exclusively driven by gains among a specific group of workers in management, natural and applied sciences and business and finance." Not broad-based across the distribution — which challenges the "tight across the whole economy" narrative.

Source: Indeed Hiring Lab Canada, [hiringlab.org/en-ca/2023/09/06/indeed-job-posting-and-wage-tracker-summer-2023/](https://www.hiringlab.org/en-ca/2023/09/06/indeed-job-posting-and-wage-tracker-summer-2023/)

### 2.3 Voluntary Turnover / Quit Rates

Canada did not experience the US "Great Resignation" at comparable magnitude. The US Great Resignation was partly structural (mismatch between COVID-era preferences and employer expectations) and partly genuine worker confidence (if V/U is high, workers know they can find alternatives). In Canada:

- Voluntary turnover rose from ~11.6% (2020) to ~15.9% (2021) and ~12.4% (2022), then to ~15.5% (2023).
- These figures come from private HR sources (Mercer), not StatsCan direct-measurement series, so treat as directional.
- Worker mobility in Canada is lower than the US structurally (housing costs, geographic labour market segmentation, lower labour reallocation per Fortin). Fortin found labour reallocation fell ~15% below 2019 levels by 2022-2024 — the opposite of what you'd expect if workers were confidently switching jobs.

A formal Canadian quit rate series comparable to JOLTS does not exist in the public StatsCan data. This is a genuine measurement gap. The BoC's SAN 2024-8 refers to "job separation rates" as one benchmark indicator but does not publish a standalone quit rate series.

**Implication**: The absence of a Great Resignation in Canada, combined with *declining* labour mobility per Fortin, is inconsistent with the "workers feel they have alternatives" worker-side tightness signal. The US worker-side story (people quitting aggressively because alternatives were abundant) simply did not play out in Canada at the same scale.

## 3. Historical Data — What Longer Series Would Tell Us

### 3.1 Canada Help-Wanted Index (1962–2003)

Statistics Canada published the Help-Wanted Index annually (1962–2002) and monthly (1962–2003) before discontinuing it. The discontinuation was explicitly because the index's performance "was no longer reflective of unmet labor demand due to the internet" — i.e., the methodological break was recognised by StatsCan at the time.

Source: StatsCan catalogue [71-540-X](https://www150.statcan.gc.ca/n1/en/catalogue/71-540-X)

**Coverage and calibration value**: The HWI covers the late-1980s Canadian labour market overheat — when wages genuinely surged 6%+ and inflation took off — as well as the 1990-91 recession and the long 1991-2000 recovery. If the HWI could be linked to the post-2015 JVWS (using a scaling approach like Diamond and Sahin 2014 for the US), we could calibrate what "tight" looked like in the 1987-1990 episode when real wages genuinely rose. That would give the 2018-2019 and 2022 episodes a very different reference frame. No such linked Canadian series has been published by StatsCan.

**Synthetic estimates**: Denton, Feaver and Robb constructed synthetic estimates for 1951–1975 combining Job Vacancy Survey data, HWI, and National Employment Service vacancy series. These are unpublished academic data; not suitable for dashboard calibration but relevant context for long-run analysis.

**Pre-2015 JVWS**: No quarterly or monthly vacancy rate series equivalent to JVWS exists for Canada pre-2015. The JVWS began as a quarterly survey in 2015 and converted to monthly in October 2020 (coinciding with COVID restart). The framework already notes this gap; it is confirmed by StatsCan documentation (SDDS 5217).

**Bottom line on history**: We cannot directly calibrate "tight" against the late-1980s Canadian overheat using vacancy data. The HWI methodology break (newspaper help-wanted ads vs internet job postings) makes direct linking unreliable even if both series overlap. This is a fundamental limitation of the framework's empirical bands, not something that can be resolved with available public data. The bands are calibrated against 10 years of data (2015–2026), of which ~3 years are anomalous COVID/post-COVID years and another 2–3 years are the post-COVID immigration surge distortion.

### 3.2 Manpower Survey of Employment Intentions

The Manpower Employment Outlook Survey (now ManpowerGroup) has tracked employer hiring intentions in Canada continuously from approximately 1962. It is an employer-survey instrument (demand-side) rather than a vacancy count. Not suitable as a V/U numerator substitute; relevant as a long-run employer-sentiment indicator. Continuous data is paywalled; limited historical abstracts are publicly accessible.

## 4. International V/U Comparisons

### 4.1 United States — Deeper Look

**JOLTS history**: JOLTS data begins December 2000 — the earliest reliable US V/U series. Pre-2000, researchers (Diamond and Sahin 2014; Barnichon 2010) extended using the Composite Help-Wanted Index scaled to match December 2000. This gives a US Beveridge curve back to ~1951 — useful context but not directly translatable to Canada.

**US 2022 tightness**: At the US V/U peak of ~2.0 (spring 2022), US nominal wage growth hit ~6.5% — the highest in 40 years. Summers and Domash ([NBER WP 29739](https://www.nber.org/papers/w29739)) constructed a "firm-side unemployment" measure and found it corresponded to a tightness level historically associated with sub-2% unemployment. **The US 2022 supply-demand validation is strong: high V/U coincided with genuine wage surge.** This is the key difference between US 2022 and Canada 2022. Canada's V/U peak was ~0.99 vs US ~2.0, AND Canada's real wages were negative vs US real wages which fell but with a much stronger nominal response.

**Post-2022 US Beveridge curve debate**: Federal Reserve research (Cleveland Fed, 2024; Chicago Fed WP 2023-38; Fed FEDS 2024) found that post-pandemic US Beveridge curve dynamics resulted from a mix of faster matching efficiency recovery and lower nominal price/wage stickiness than previous decades. The key finding: the vacancy decline from peak did not require the unemployment surge that prior Beveridge-curve-based models predicted, supporting a "soft landing" (transitory matching friction) interpretation. Applied to Canada: if the 2022 outward shift was partly matching-friction rather than genuine excess demand, the rapid V/U normalisation post-2023 does not necessarily mean the labour market was wrong to describe 2022 as tight — but it does suggest the V/U-based tightness measure overstated genuine structural tightness relative to what wage-based measures implied.

**Summers' contribution**: Summers/Domash's firm-side unemployment concept specifically addressed the problem of relying solely on the unemployment rate as a tightness measure. Their argument was that V/U-based measures were *better* predictors of wage inflation than unemployment alone. This is relevant to our framework in reverse: if V/U is theoretically a better predictor of wage pressure than unemployment, and yet Canadian V/U at ~0.99 coincided with real wage declines, that's a more fundamental inconsistency than just a label problem.

### 4.2 United Kingdom

UK ONS Vacancies Series begins 2001 (Vacancy Survey); the series has long history and good coverage. At the UK V/U peak in 2022:
- Unemployment-to-vacancies ratio hit record lows (vacancy rate ~4.9% in Q2 2022).
- The labour market "tightened on all three headline measures: the unemployment rate was close to record lows, the unemployment-to-vacancies ratio was at record levels, and workers were moving jobs at record rates."
- Wage growth was expected to reach "just under 5%" in 2022 — but real wages were sharply negative because CPI peaked at 11.1% in October 2022.

Source: Resolution Foundation ([resolutionfoundation.org/comment/a-tightening-labour-market-wont-spare-workers-a-big-pay-squeeze-in-2022/](https://www.resolutionfoundation.org/comment/a-tightening-labour-market-wont-spare-workers-a-big-pay-squeeze-in-2022/)); Bank of England ([bankofengland.co.uk](https://www.bankofengland.co.uk/bank-overground/2022/what-factors-drove-underlying-pay-growth-in-2021))

**Bank of England characterisation**: The Bank of England's MPC treated the V/U signal as materially above employment-side unemployment-gap signals and used it to justify a more hawkish stance than the unemployment gap alone would support. The BoE's Jonathan Haskel speech (November 2023) directly addressed wage inflation persistence vs the V/U tightness signal — noting wage growth "may be more persistent than the vacancy decline implied." This is the exact analytical question for Canada too.

**International comparison**: UK's V/U peak was proportionally similar to Canada's when normalised to pre-pandemic levels — both roughly doubled from pre-pandemic. Both produced real wage declines despite high nominal vacancy counts. The UK story doesn't challenge the "tight" narrative so much as confirm that "tight" on V/U measures did not protect workers' purchasing power in a high-inflation environment.

### 4.3 Australia

Australia's 2022 episode is the strongest international cross-validation case for genuine tightness:
- Unemployment reached a 48-year low of 3.4% (October 2022); underemployment fell to 5.9% (early 2023, lowest since 2000).
- Vacancies peaked May 2022; "workers were more likely to find work in 2022 than at any point since data began in 2004."
- Wage growth peaked at ~4.3% Y/Y (December 2023). Real wages fell in 2022 due to CPI (~7%), then recovered in 2023–2024 as inflation fell faster than wages moderated.
- The RBA explicitly used both employer-side (job vacancies, employment intentions) and worker-side (job switching rates, underemployment) signals in its tightness assessment and treated the 2022 episode as substantive tightness.
- The RBA's April 2024 Bulletin: "higher wages as they struggle to fill vacancies and experience high staff turnover" — explicitly naming job switching (a worker-side signal) alongside vacancies (employer-side).

Sources: RBA Bulletin April 2024, [rba.gov.au/publications/bulletin/2024/apr/assessing-full-employment-in-australia.html](https://www.rba.gov.au/publications/bulletin/2024/apr/assessing-full-employment-in-australia.html); AMP, "Econosights — How tight is Australia's labour market"

**Australia's lesson for Canada**: Australia in 2022 had both high V/U *and* high job switching rates, high quit rates, and sustained nominal wage gains that eventually recovered real purchasing power as inflation fell. Canada in 2022 had high V/U but *lower* job switching (Fortin), no equivalent quit surge, and real wage declines that did not recover until inflation fell two years later. The Canadian worker-side experience was materially weaker than Australia's, supporting the hypothesis that Canada's 2022 V/U overstated genuine worker-bargaining-power tightness relative to Australia's.

### 4.4 European Union and Germany

Eurostat Job Vacancy Statistics (JVS) cover EU member states from ~2004 (with good cross-country comparability from ~2006). The EU job vacancy rate peaked in Q3-Q4 2022 at ~3.1% (euro area). The IMF's [SDN 2022/001: "Labor Market Tightness in Advanced Economies"](https://www.imf.org/-/media/Files/Publications/SDN/2022/English/SDNEA2022001.ashx) found:

- By late 2021: 50–80% more unfilled jobs in Australia, Canada, UK, and US than pre-pandemic.
- Germany and Japan showed *lower* V/U ratios than in 2019 even as other economies surged — consistent with Germany's structural labour market institutions (Kurzarbeit short-time work) absorbing shocks differently.
- "Labor market tightness raises wage growth across the board, but more than twice as much in low-pay sectors than in others."

The IMF SDN specifically identifies three contributing factors to vacancy surges: (1) barriers to returning to work, (2) changing worker preferences away from certain job types, and (3) sectoral/occupational mismatch — all of which complicate the pure demand-excess interpretation of elevated V/U.

**Germany context**: Germany's V/U ratio hit all-time highs since 2015 per the ifo Institute's labour shortage indicator, but Germany's *overall* V/U did not show the same COVID-era spike as English-speaking economies. German unemployment has been rising since 2022 "due in addition to immigration from Ukraine to economic weakness." Germany's institutional labour market design (strong unions, sectoral wage bargaining) means employer-reported shortages in Germany do translate more directly to wage outcomes than in Canada — making German BOS-equivalent data more reliable as a tightness signal.

## 7. Recommendation: Which Option is Most Defensible

**Option A+B is most defensible.** Here is the specific framing:

### Why not Option C (hard two-tier requiring both V/U and wage pressure)?

A hard two-tier requiring V/U AND wage-pressure confirmation before firing is analytically cleanest and most defensible against the empirical evidence. However, it creates a practical problem: wage-pressure confirmation requires one of the four wage measures to exceed 3% Y/Y (or real wages to be positive and above the historical median). In the 2018–2019 period, wages were growing ~2.5% nominally (below 3%) and real wages were flat. Under a strict two-tier filter, the 2018–2019 episode would not have registered as "tight" — but BoC was genuinely worried about overheating and was actively hiking. Denying the tightness label entirely in that episode would misrepresent BoC's real-time assessment in a way the dashboard should not do. Option C is analytically purer but risks making the dashboard's labour tightness characterisation *less* useful as a BoC-tracking tool.

### Why not just keep current labels with a caveat?

The current labels have two specific problems:
1. The "starting to be tight" anchor in the same speech that documents the wage-growth puzzle is a known defect, not just a caveat. Using the Wilkins quote as an anchor while not surfacing Wilkins' own qualification about wage growth is misleading.
2. The labels are being used (implicitly, via the immigration-policy loop section in the report's prose) as if they represent verified economic tightness. They represent verified *employer-complaint tightness* — a meaningful but narrower concept.

### Option A+B — Proposed Retitling

| V/U Band | Proposed Label | Rationale |
|---|---|---|

The **prose cross-check** (the +B component): whenever V/U fires a label above "near-balance," the blurb generation framework should surface whether LFS-Micro wage growth confirms or qualifies the reading. Specifically: if V/U > 0.45 but LFS-Micro < 3% and real wages negative, the prose should note "employer vacancy counts elevated, but wage growth is not yet confirming labour-market tightness in the textbook sense." This preserves the diagnostic utility (V/U is tracking something real) while not over-asserting tightness from a single demand-side signal.

## 8. Key Gaps in Available Evidence

1. **No Canadian quit rate series.** A formal voluntary-separation rate comparable to US JOLTS quits is not publicly available from StatsCan. This is the most important missing supply-side signal. The JVWS Guide 75-514-G mentions "reasons for vacancy" but no public quit-rate series exists at the economy-wide level for Canada. LFS transition-flow data (Table 14-10-0125) can partially substitute but is not in the dashboard.

2. **No pre-2015 vacancy series linkable to JVWS.** The HWI break at 2003 (internet disruption to newspaper-ad-based measurement) prevents direct extension. A Canadian V/U series for the 1987-1990 overheat would provide the strongest calibration anchor — wages genuinely surged 5–6%, real wages rose, inflation took off. That historical episode would let us ask "what was V/U when the labour market was unambiguously tight by all measures?" without that, the bands are calibrated against an era that includes no genuinely unconstrained tight episode.

3. **JVWS methodology on vacancy definition.** The JVWS definition of "job vacancy" (StatsCan SDDS 5217) covers positions "actively trying to fill" with specific requirements — but does not appear to distinguish between vacancies where the employer is offering below-market wages vs. vacancies where genuine scarcity prevents filling. This is a measurement gap that no public StatsCan documentation addresses explicitly.

4. **Fortin's regional Beveridge curves are descriptive, not causal.** Fortin documents the outward shift and attributes it to matching inefficiency, but does not rigorously separate matching-efficiency degradation from pure demand-side over-heating. The two are entangled in vacancy data.

5. **Immigration's wage suppression effect is not quantified for the 2022-2024 period specifically.** Skuterud's endogeneity argument (labour demand is endogenous to supply) is compelling theoretically but we do not have a published Canadian empirical estimate of how much the 2022-2024 immigration surge suppressed wage growth below what would otherwise have been observed. This would be the key empirical number to resolve the "was V/U >0.80 genuinely tight given the labour supply expansion?" question.
